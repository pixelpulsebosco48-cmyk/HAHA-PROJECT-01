Reading Passage 1

The Global Journey of Coffee

A. The origins of coffee are steeped in legend and folklore, most notably the tale of Kaldi, a 9th-century Ethiopian goatherd. According to popular myth, Kaldi discovered coffee after noticing that his goats became unusually energetic after eating berries from a certain tree. Whether true or not, historical records show that the cultivation and trade of coffee began on the Arabian Peninsula. By the 15th century, coffee was being grown in the Yemeni district of Arabia and by the 16th century it was known in Persia, Egypt, Syria, and Turkey.

B. Coffee reached Europe in the 17th century, where it was initially met with suspicion or religious prejudice. Some local clergy in Venice condemned it as the "bitter invention of Satan." However, with the approval of Pope Clement VIII, who found the drink satisfying, coffeehouses rapidly spread across Europe. These establishments became centers of social activity and communication in major cities. In England, they were dubbed "penny universities" because for the price of a penny, one could purchase a cup of coffee and engage in stimulating conversation.

C. As demand for the beverage continued to grow, there was fierce competition to cultivate coffee outside of Arabia. The Dutch finally succeeded in the latter half of the 17th century. They obtained seedlings and successfully planted them in their colonies in Indonesia, specifically on the island of Java. The Dutch later expanded the cultivation of coffee trees to the islands of Sumatra and Celebes, breaking the Arab monopoly and transforming coffee into a truly global agricultural commodity.

Reading Passage 2

Mars: The Next Frontier

A. For decades, humanity has looked to Mars as the most viable candidate for extraterrestrial colonization. With a day length nearly identical to Earth's and evidence of ancient riverbeds suggesting past water flow, the Red Planet has captured the imagination of astronomers and the public alike. However, the surface of Mars is a hostile environment. It possesses a thin atmosphere composed mostly of carbon dioxide, offering little protection from harsh solar radiation.

B. Modern space agencies, including NASA and the ESA, have deployed numerous rovers to analyze the Martian soil and atmosphere. The discovery of subsurface ice in the planet's polar regions has been a breakthrough. Water is heavy and expensive to transport from Earth, so the ability to extract it directly from Mars is crucial for any long-term human settlement. This water could theoretically be split into hydrogen and oxygen to produce rocket fuel and breathable air.

C. One of the largest hurdles to Martian colonization is the physiological toll of long-duration space travel. A one-way trip to Mars takes roughly seven to nine months using current propulsion technology. During this time, astronauts would experience prolonged microgravity, which leads to muscle atrophy and severe bone density loss, regardless of strict exercise regimes. Addressing these medical challenges is currently as high a priority as developing the rockets to get there.

Reading Passage 3

The Rise of AI in Creative Arts

A. Artificial intelligence (AI) has made unprecedented strides in fields historically considered exclusively human, most notably the creative arts. In recent years, generative AI models have demonstrated the ability to compose classical music, write poetry, and generate hyper-realistic digital paintings in a matter of seconds. These algorithms operate by processing massive datasets of human-created art, identifying complex patterns, and synthesizing entirely new works based on text prompts.

B. The rapid advancement of algorithmic art has sparked an intense philosophical and legal debate. Proponents argue that AI democratizes art, allowing individuals without formal technical training to express their creative visions. Conversely, professional artists have expressed deep concerns regarding copyright infringement. Because these AI models are trained on millions of copyrighted images scoured from the internet without the original creators' consent, many argue that generative art is inherently derivative and ethically problematic.

C. Despite the controversy, major industries are rapidly adopting generative technologies. In the video game industry, developers use AI to rapidly generate background textures and non-player character dialogue, drastically cutting down production times. Similarly, advertising agencies are utilizing AI to create custom, targeted visual campaigns. The future of creative work is unlikely to be a total replacement of human artists, but rather a hybrid collaborative model where humans act as 'curators' of AI-generated concepts.
